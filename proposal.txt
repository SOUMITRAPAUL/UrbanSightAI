ProjectTitle:UrbanSightAI
Data-Driven Governance for Resilient Cities

1. Introduction & Problem Statement
Rapid urbanization in Bangladesh has exposed governance shortfalls that are the principal
obstacle to realizing SDG-11 at the local level. Core municipal functions,drainage,road
maintenance, land-use regulation, utilities and emergency response are split among multiple
agencies (city corporations,RAJUK,WASA,LGED), producing overlapping mandates, duplicated
investments and slow inter-agency handoffs that stall implementation at the ward level [1][4]. At
the same time, municipal fiscal autonomy is weak and planning capacity remains limited; critical
ward-scale operational data are fragmented, outdated, or inaccessible to the staff who must
make budget decisions [2][5]. Informal settlements where vulnerability concentrates are
frequently unmapped or excluded from routine plans, leaving large populations uncounted in
resource allocation and exposed to repeated service failures (waterlogging, fire risk, sanitation
gaps) [14][15].
This is a governance problem more than a technical one: councils and engineers rarely lack
willing action; they lack trusted, ward-level evidence, standardized decision products, and
straightforward budget-ready justification that fit municipal procurement and financing cycles.
Without these policy instruments, authorities default to reactive, costly interventions rather than
preventive, prioritized investments that reduce exposure and deliver durable SDG-11 outcomes
[1][20]. UrbanSightAI is explicitly designed to close that gap by converting spatial data and field
verification into budget-aware, interagency-ready policy outputs (Top-N worklists, costed
memos, SDG-11 ward cards) municipal teams can adopt immediately [17][18][19].

2. Motivation & Rationale
Policy impact requires alignment of three elements: evidence, decision rules, and financing. In
Bangladesh, two persistent bottlenecks impede that alignment: (a) inconsistent or inaccessible
ward-level evidence and (b) constrained, opaque municipal finance that slows targeted action
[2][5]. UrbanSightAI is designed to bridge these gaps with a “policy-first” architecture: AI and
geospatial analytics are used not as ends but as translators converting imagery and reports into
plain-language, costed, implementable actions that match municipal workflows and budget
cycles [1][13].
Why this matters for policy. Simple, low-cost micro-interventions (drain cleaning, short road
repairs, waste hotspot clearance, targeted tree planting) deliver outsized returns when allocated
to the right places. But planners lack a repeatable, auditable method to pick the “right places.”
UrbanSightAI provides that method: it produces ranked, equity-weighted intervention lists,
shows the expected beneficiaries, flags permits and responsible agencies, and packages all this

3

into procurement-ready briefs for finance and engineering teams. Evidence from municipal
observatories and participatory platforms shows that decision-support tools which produce
exportable, accountable policy products are the ones that get used in budgets and council
meetings; the design therefore prioritizes usability and defensibility over maximum technical
sophistication [11][13][8].
Operationally, the pilot uses light, accessible data (Sentinel/Landsat, municipal shapefiles or
OSM fallbacks, and focused field labeling of ~200–400 geotagged points) so municipalities can
implement quickly without heavy sensor investments [17][18][19]. Civic validation (Bangla
chatbot + participatory portal) is embedded so community priorities appear as weighted inputs in
ranking increasing legitimacy and reducing political friction [8][10]. Economically, prioritizing
micro-works reduces repeated emergency spending and creates clear, data-based cases for
reallocation or donor support, a pragmatic path to scale that speaks directly to finance officers
and councillors [5][6]. The pilot will demonstrate measurable policy wins (reduced
detection-to-workorder time; share of Top-N items adopted), creating a replicable model for
SDG-11 localization across Bangladeshi cities [1][20].

3. Background Study / Related Works (policy-oriented
synthesis)
UrbanSightAI draws on three strands with direct policy relevance.
SDG localization & municipal finance:UN inter-agency policy briefs and national reviews
emphasize that SDG delivery is local by design: achieving SDG targets requires empowered
municipal governments with interoperable data, fiscal tools, and transparent prioritization
mechanisms [1][2][20]. Studies of subnational finance document limited own-source revenue
and dependency on central transfers, which delays action even when priorities are known,
underscoring the need for tools that improve the case for timely municipal spending [5][4].
Operational urban platforms & participatory governance:Global practitioner cases show
how fused data plus governance-focused outputs change municipal behavior. Jakarta’s flood
dashboard and city observatories illustrate how data integration informs operational
decision-making; Decidim and civic chatbots demonstrate how structured citizen input can be
turned into traceable policy signals used in budgeting and planning [11][13][8][9][10]. The core
lesson: dashboards that remain purely informational rarely change budgets but systems that
produce simulation outputs, procurement-ready briefs and participatory validation do affect
policy.
Technical evidence for implementable indicators:Remote-sensing and ML research confirm
that feasible, operational indicators (informal settlement footprint, blocked-drain counts,
green-deficit indices) can be generated from multispectral imagery when paired with targeted
field validation and simple spatial joins [14][15]. Short-horizon predictors and change-detection

4

methods add timing and evidence for urgency, but their policy value depends on packaging into
budgeted, interagency actions rather than raw forecasts [16]. Few projects close this last mile:
convert mapping into cost, equity-aware intervention lists and interagency coordination packets
which is precisely the gap UrbanSightAI addresses by design [12][14][15].

4.0 Proposed Solution Technical Methodology.
UrbanSightAI is a policy-first platform designed to:
i. Equip municipal decision-makers with a ward-level digital twin that summarizes
assets, service gaps, exposure and governance constraints.​
ii. Produce ranked, cost policy actions (micro-works) formatted for budget requests,
procurement and engineering work orders.​
iii. Run scenario simulations that show budget → outcome tradeoffs (impact, equity,
timeline).​
iv. Integrate citizen evidence (chatbot / participatory portal) so community priorities and
complaints directly influence policy ranks.​
v. Provide automated SDG-11 governance cards, inter-agency packets and audit trails
for transparency and donor reporting.

AI Components policy tools (what they do, and what they output)

5

1.​ Prioritization & Decision Ranker (core)​

What it is: A ranker (LightGBM / XGBoost or logistic ranker) that scores
candidate projects by impact-per-cost, feasibility and equity weighting.​

Policy output: Ordered “Top-N” worklist with simple justifications (people
helped, cost, why now).​

2.​ Policy Scenario Simulator (prescriptive engine)​

What it is: Lightweight simulator that tests counterfactuals (allocate X
BDT → predicted reduction in affected households).​

Policy output: Budget comparison tables and one-page memos for
finance officers.​

3.​ Evidence Extractor (segmentation → indicators)​

What it is: Segmentation model (U-Net/DeepLab) + spatial joins that
convert imagery and vectors into policy indicators (area informal, blocked
drains count, green deficit).​

Policy output: Standardized indicators used in ranking and reports.​

4.​ Civic Intelligence (NLP + Chatbot)​

What it is: Bangla NLP pipeline / lightweight LLM that classifies citizen
reports, sentiment and priority signals.​

Policy output: Validation flags and community-priority scores that nudge
rankings and create community-facing summary pages.​

6

5.​ Governance Summarizer (generative brief generator)​

What it is: Template-based generative module that converts data +
ranking into editable policy briefs and procurement notes.​

Policy output: Ready-to-sign one-page briefs, procurement checklists
and donor-ready charts.

Technical Workflow concise, policy-oriented steps
1.​ Data intake: ingest satellite, ward GIS, asset registers, census/gridded pop, past
workorders, citizen reports.​
2.​ Preprocess & standardize: tile imagery, compute indices, align vectors and tag
provenance.​
3.​ Evidence extraction: run segmentation and anomaly detectors → compute ward
indicators (exposed population, service gaps).​
4.​ Candidate generation: auto-propose feasible micro-works (jurisdiction, permit
flag, rough unit cost).​

7

5.​ Rank & simulate: Prioritization Ranker orders candidates; Scenario Simulator
runs budget tests and equity impacts.​
6.​ Produce policy packets: Top-N list → one-page brief + interagency checklist +
export (CSV/PDF) for procurement.​
7.​ Field & civic validation: mobile verification + chatbot feedback update scores;
system logs actions for audit and retraining.​

Full-Scale Future Implementation (beyond course pilot)
i. Deploy microservices for resilient, multi-ward scaling.​
ii. Integrate with municipal financial systems and procurement APIs for end-to-end
budget workflows.​
iii. Expand indicator library (health, mobility, air/heat metrics) and multi-season training
data.​
iv. Federate municipal datasets with national LGD/RAJUK resources under MOUs.​
v. Add role-based governance console (finance, engineering, councillor views) and
meeting-ready export packs.​
vi. Institutionalize a small UrbanSightAI policy unit inside city government to steward
data, priorities and model updates.​
vii. Offer managed service model (municipal subscription + capacity building) to fund
continued operations.

5.0 Scope and Pilot Implementation
Project Scope
i. Build the core UrbanSightAI system with an AI Prioritization engine, Digital-Twin assembly,
Bangla chatbot (citizen input), and a Policy Scenario Simulator.​
ii. Provide chatbot interfaces and citizen-facing support (chatbot messages, participatory portal
summaries, and simple field forms).​
iii. Deliver a web planner dashboard for ward selectors, SDG-11 cards, exportable Top-N
worklists and policy packets.​
iv. Train and validate models using small-scale, locally collected datasets in the pilot (satellite
patches, 200–400 geotagged field photos, basic municipal layers).

Pilot Implementation (For This Course)
For the course pilot we will implement a ward-scale, policy-focused proof-of-concept with these
concrete components:
1.​ Basic web dashboard & user registration role-based login (planner / enumerator /
viewer), ward selector, last-update stamp, CSV/PDF export.​
2.​ Pilot Prioritization Model a lightweight ranking model (LightGBM baseline) that scores
candidate micro-works by impact-per-cost and equity; outputs a Top-10 worklist.​
3.​ Segmentation & Evidence Extractor (pilot) U-Net/transfer-learning pilot that maps
informal patches and generates standardized ward indicators (exposed area,
blocked-drain count).​
4.​ Policy Scenario Simulator (mini) simple budget→impact simulator that tests 2–3
counterfactuals and produces one-page memos for finance officers.​
5.​ Chatbot & Field Data Pipe basic NLP classifier for citizen reports (blocked drain, flood,
waste) plus an ODK/KoBo form; includes a notification/simulation console to demo
stakeholder flows.​

7. Impact on Society
UrbanSightAI strengthens urban governance by enabling data-grounded policy, fair
budgeting, and transparent decision-making. It will:
i. Improve ward-level planning through actionable, quantified insights.​
ii. Reduce resource waste by replacing reactive fixing with evidence-based
prioritization.​
iii. Enable equitable development by highlighting underserved zones in policy ranking.​
iv. Increase transparency through citizen participation and audit-ready decision logs.​
v. Support faster response, better spending, and fairer city service distribution.
This leads to more accountable institutions, safer urban environments, and resilient
growth.

9.0 Revenue Plan & Sustainability
UrbanSightAI is designed to be socially useful and financially sustainable. The revenue
model combines municipal subscriptions, data services, consultancy, grants and light
civic premium options. Each stream keeps basic civic access free while generating
stable income to fund operations, model updates and scale.

1. Municipal Subscription (Core B2G / B2City)
What: Tiered annual subscriptions by city size / wards (basic → standard → enterprise).
Includes dashboard access, Top-N worklists, scenario simulator, API access and admin
training.​
Why it generates revenue: City budgets already fund planning tools; a small line-item
for data-driven prioritization is cost-justified by improved budget efficiency and reduced
emergency spend.

2. Data-as-a-Service (DaaS) & API Access
What: Pay-per-dataset or subscription API (ward indicators, historical exposure maps,
time-series exports) for planners, researchers and NGOs.​
Why: Researchers and consultancies pay for clean, processed urban datasets;
recurring API fees scale with usage.

3. Consultancy & Integration Services
What: One-time fees for integration (procurement packet automation), training
workshops, MOU facilitation, and custom model tuning.​

14

Why: Municipalities lack in-house capacity; paid integration reduces adoption friction
and funds local onboarding.

4. Grants & Development Funding
What: Seed grants and implementation funding from donors, development banks, and
UN agencies for pilot and scale phases.​
Why: Development partners favor measurable SDG tools; grants lower municipal
up-front costs and de-risk adoption.

5. Performance-Based / Results Contracts
What: Contracts that tie fees to agreed outcomes (e.g., % of Top-N actions adopted,
reduction in detection→workorder time).​
Why: Demonstrates value and aligns incentives donors or city units pay more when
measurable improvements occur.

6. Civic Premium Services (Optional & Low-Cost)
What: Basic citizen reporting and dashboard remain free. Optional paid civic features
(neighborhood alerts via SMS, advanced historical reports for NGOs) offered at low
cost.​
Why: Maintains accessibility while monetizing high-value convenience features for local
NGOs and community groups.

7. Ethical Commercial Partnerships
What: Aggregated, anonymized analytics for utilities, insurers, or real-estate firms
(non-sensitive, opt-in). No individual-level or household-identifying commercial sales.​
Why: Private sector will pay for city-level risk and exposure analytics if privacy and
ethics are preserved.

8. Training & Certification Programs
What: Paid certification for municipal staff and consultants in using UrbanSightAI
workflows and interpreting outputs.​
Why: Helps institutionalize use and creates a recurring training revenue stream.

15

9. White-label / Licensing
What: License the platform (or parts) to regional consultancies or NGOs who want a
branded deployment.​
Why: Accelerates geographic scale without full in-house expansion.

10. Expected Outcomes and Deliverables
1. AI Policy Prioritization Output
An advanced ranking model that optimizes micro-works by cost-efficiency and social equity, providing planners with evidence-based justifications.
2. Global AI Expert Assistant
An English-language RAG interface that translates complex data and citizen signals into structured policy insights.
3. Ward-Level Digital Twin Dashboard
High-fidelity spatial analytics for real-time visualization of service gaps, infrastructure conditions, and flood risks.
4. Budget Scenario & Policy Recommendation Engine
A prescriptive simulation tool that forecasts project outcomes per BDT spent, ensuring maximum impact for every budget allocation.
5. Unified Planner Console

Backend APIs connect all AI components into one cohesive governance tool.
